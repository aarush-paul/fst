Problem or poor management.

What is overtourism?

Overtourism refers to a scenario where there are too many visitors to a particular destination. This excessive influx often results in degradation of the environment, infrastructure strain, diminished quality of life for residents, and a poor visitor experience. Overtourism has become a prominent issue in various global destinations, including cities like Venice and Barcelona, or natural sites like Mount Everest and the Galápagos Islands. The term ‘overtourism’ is relatively new, having been coined over a decade ago to highlight the spiralling numbers of visitors taking a toll on cities, landmarks and landscapes. Overtourism is arguably a social phenomenon too. In China and India, two of the most populated countries where space is at a premium, crowded places are socially accepted and overtourism concerns are rarely articulated, if at all. This suggests that cultural expectations of personal space and expectations of exclusivity differ.

Overcrowding in India’s popular tourist destinations has become a significant problem recently. As a result of an increase in the number of people who travel and the development of the tourism industry, several vital locations in India need help to accommodate the growing number of tourists. Because of this, there is now an excessive amount of people present, which creates issues not just for the vacationers but also for the native people and the environment. The mountain ranges of India are just one example of the country’s great variety, and the country also has a significant cultural legacy. Consequently, it has developed into a well-liked vacation spot for people worldwide. Nevertheless, due to growing popularity, many popular tourist spots are now overrun with visitors, which has a wide range of harmful impacts.

Example

Sikkim, which has a population of about 600,000, has also seen a significant increase in tourists in the last decade. In 2011, 552,000 domestic tourists visited. In 2019, these tourists increased to more than 1.4 million.






Himachal Pradesh received 14.9 million tourists in 2011, whose number increased to 17.2 million in 2019. Tourist visits continued even in 2020 at the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic and over three million tourists visited the Himalayan region.
